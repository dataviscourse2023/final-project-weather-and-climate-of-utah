Orographic Gradients

In the mountainous western United States, terrain significantly influences precipitation totals and temperature. Typically, precipitation will increase with elevation due to high terrain, forcing the precipitating air masses to ascend. The forced ascent will cause the air mass to cool, increase condensation and the formulation of raindrops, and then precipitation totals will increase. This creates a strong contrast between the relatively wetter mountainous regions and the drier valleys.


Temperatures decrease with elevation due to the decrease in air pressure, which allows air to expand and causes a cooling effect. On a global average, temperature decreases altitudinally by 6.5°C per kilometer. This rate decreases when observing temperatures along a slope due to the mountain surface warming the air above it. Previous research in the western United States has estimated the rate of decrease in temperature with elevation to range between 3.9°C and 5.2°C per kilometer.

Wildfires and Warm Season Temperatures

During the warm-season months (defined as May - October), increased temperatures can result in enhanced wildfire activity across the western continental United States. Thus, for emergency personnel, it can be helpful to know if there's a historical trend in wildfire activity with temperature to plan for increased mitigation efforts in future years. Great Salt Lake Water Elevation and Cool Season Precipitation

Cool-season precipitation totals heavily influence the water elevation of the Great Salt Lake. During the cooler months, shown here from November to April for stations within the Great Salt Lake's drainage basin, precipitation accumulates as snow in mountainous regions. This snowpack is a critical water source for the lake as it gradually melts in the warmer months, contributing to the Great Salt Lake's water level. However, variations in cool-season precipitation greatly impact the lake's elevation. Years with above-average snowfall lead to increased freshwater inflow to the lake, elevating its levels and supporting the migratory bird population. Conversely, drier winters result in reduced inflow, potentially causing a decline in water elevation, altering the lake's shoreline, and increasing the lake's salinity to the detriment of the brine shrimp and migratory birds.
