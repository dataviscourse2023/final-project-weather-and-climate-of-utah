Great Salt Lake Water Elevation and Cool Season Precipitation

Cool-season precipitation totals heavily influence the water elevation of the Great Salt Lake. During the cooler months, shown here from November to April for stations within the Great Salt Lake's drainage basin, precipitation accumulates as snow in mountainous regions. This snowpack is a critical water source for the lake as it gradually melts in the warmer months, contributing to the Great Salt Lake's water level. However, variations in cool-season precipitation greatly impact the lake's elevation. Years with above-average snowfall lead to increased freshwater inflow to the lake, elevating its levels and supporting the migratory bird population. Conversely, drier winters result in reduced inflow, potentially causing a decline in water elevation and altering the lake's shoreline, increasing the lake's salinity to the detriment of the brine shrimp and migratory birds.
